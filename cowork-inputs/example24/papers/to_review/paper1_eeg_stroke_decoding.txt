Title: Decoding Motor Intention from EEG During Stroke Rehabilitation: Real-World Performance Across Clinical Settings
Authors: Hoffmann, R., Nakamura, K., Singh, P., Osei, B.
Journal: Brain Communications, 2026
DOI: 10.1093/braincomms/fcad089

HYPOTHESIS
EEG-based motor intention decoders trained in laboratory settings will show significant performance
degradation when deployed in clinical rehabilitation environments due to artefact and patient variability.

METHODOLOGY
Prospective multi-site study. 45 stroke survivors at 3 rehabilitation hospitals.
Each participant completed 12 sessions: 4 lab, 8 clinical.
Decoder trained on lab data; tested on clinical data (different days, clinical environment).
Primary outcome: classification accuracy (idle vs motor attempt), measured as AUC.

KEY RESULTS
- Lab AUC: 0.87 (SD 0.09) — expected from prior literature
- Clinical AUC: 0.79 (SD 0.14) — significant drop (p<0.001)
- Performance drop correlated with: electrode impedance, ambient EMG noise, patient fatigue
- Adaptive decoder (online update): clinical AUC recovered to 0.85 after 2 sessions of adaptation

LIMITATIONS
- Relatively small sample (45 patients) from 3 sites — may not generalise
- All sites in Germany — cultural/healthcare context may affect patient engagement
- Adaptive decoder requires 2 additional sessions — practical burden not assessed

RELEVANCE TO: Neural interfaces in motor rehabilitation — HIGH
Critical paper for my RCT design. The 8-point AUC drop from lab to clinic is exactly the challenge
I need to address with personalised decoders in FLF fellowship application.
